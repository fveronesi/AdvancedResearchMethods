Introduction

Statistical analysis of hate interpretation of Tweets based on geographical location, commissioned by a client on Upwork.com.

Example of data science, applied to a topic where I do not have any domain knowledge.

Therefore I worked closely with the client to make sure of producing robust results.

Research questions

The client had several research questions:

  • Does country affect hate interpretation?

  • Does continent affect hate interpretation?

  • Which countries/continents are most/least sensitive to on-line hate?

Experimental Design

Tweets were shown randomly to people around the world.

Each person would rate the hatefulness of the tweet on a Likert scale: very hateful, hateful, moderately and slightly hateful, not hateful and not hateful at all.

Each rater would also answer questions to compute a trust score, and obtain data on country and city.

Possible Analysis

Data reported on Likert scale are generally analysed by aggregation.

For example counting the number of occurrences of a certain label per country.

Count data can then be analysed with a GLM.

Interpretation Score

To further increase our knowledge of the relation between tweets and countries, the client asked to compute the interpretation score.

This is the difference between the average rate per tweet and the individual score of a rater:

\(Interpretation Score = \mu_i - s_r\)

where \(\mu_i\) is the average score for tweet \(i\), and \(s_r\) is the score that rater \(r\) gave to the same tweet \(i\). Positive score means the rater perceived the tweet as more hateful than the majority of raters.

Conclusion

In other words, even though we found some geographical effects that may explain the differences in how on-line hate is perceived, the majority of differences are caused by non-geographical factors.

This makes sense, since it is plausible that sociological factors have more influence in the way we perceived and are affected by on-line hate.

Outlook

These insights allowed the client to first produce a conference paper, and then design a follow up experiment where they would ask raters to include information on education, standard of living ect.

This coupled with a more complex statistical model (and possibly a different sampling strategy, e.g.Â stratified) could allow them to better understand the main drivers on perception of on-line hate.
